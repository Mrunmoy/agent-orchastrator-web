Chapter: The Third Bridge

Saga Chapter · ADPT-003 · 2026-03-07

Two bridges already stood in the great hall of the Orchestrator — one reaching to the domain of Claude, silver-tongued and session-wise; the other spanning to Codex, whose nested scrolls required patient unwinding. Both carried the same seal: the Adapter Contract, forged in the earliest days of the project.

But a council of two is a debate. A council of three is a deliberation. And so the builders turned their eyes to the mountains, where the open-weight Ollama kept watch over its local models, beholden to no distant cloud.

This third bridge would be different. Ollama was no debater — it was a scribe. Its purpose was not to argue a position, but to listen to the arguments of Claude and Codex and produce a neutral memo: a summary, an analysis, a moment of clarity in the heat of structured disagreement.

And because a scribe needs no memory of past sessions, no identity across conversations, this bridge was built not as a class with state and lineage, but as a pair of simple incantations — one to summon a memo, one to check if the scribe was present at all. The prompt was whispered through a pipe, not shouted as an argument, for debate transcripts are long and full of characters that upset the shell.

Fifteen tests were inscribed before the first line of production rune-work. The tests spoke of success and failure, of timeouts and missing commands, of custom models and remote hosts. Only when all fifteen glowed red with failure did the implementation begin. And when the runes were complete, all fifteen turned green in a single pass.

The Orchestrator now commands three voices:

  • Claude, the session-keeper, who remembers and resumes;
  • Codex, the structured respondent, whose nested outputs unfold like origami;
  • Ollama, the neutral scribe, who distills debate into clarity.

With three bridges standing, the builders began to hear whispers of the next chapter — the scheduler that would decide when each voice speaks, and the event schema that would record what they said in a common tongue.
